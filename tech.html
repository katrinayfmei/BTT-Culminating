Humidity Swing Technology

To remove carbon dioxide from the air, Treepods use a revoluntionary process called humidity swing technology. It was invented by Klaus Lackner and Allen Wright, both professors at Arizona State University. The material they designed absorbs carbon dioxide when it's dry, and releases it when it's wet. We use this material in the form of an environmentally friendly, alkaline resin. The resin covers the ends of the branches and reacts with the carbon dioxide in the air, catching it and then sending it to the filtration system. The material is then washed with water and the carbon dioxide is released. Lastly, the carbon dioxide is either sent to reserves connected directly to the tree, or picked up and brought to designated underground carbon dioxide dumps.
Material

Treepods are a solution for a more sustainable, more eco-friendly future and as such, they are not made with any new material. All the materials used in the process of making a Treepod have been recycled. For example, the main body of the Treepod is made entirely out of recycled plastic water bottles. Using recycled plastic also has benefits because it can be easily processed and shaped.
Biomimicry

The design of the Treepod uses biomimicry, a process that copies natural shapes and systems from nature to help create optimal structure and efficiency. Treepods are modelled after the Dragon Blood tree, a tree whose branches create an umbrella shaped canopy; and the human lungs, specifically, the pulmonary alveoli. A Treepod's canopy follows the structure of the Dragon Blood tree, maximizing the area in which the tree projects shade and makes contact with the air, allowing it to grab more carbon dioxide. This also allows proper wind flow for sturdiness. The filter and storage system is modelled after the pulmonary alveoli structure, a special part of our respiratory system that filters air and keeps us alive.
Energy

Treepods need energy to filter the carbon dioxide and to provide lighting at night. These systems are powered through solar panels that cover the tree's canopy, and seesaws at its base that generate kinetic energy.
Social Aspect

The seesaws that generate kinetic energy promote play as well as help to educate children on the system. In using the seesaws, they are helping to create a cleaner future and providing lighting for their community. These lights allow people to be able to see or work at night when they might not have any light at home. The lights may also helps protect villagers from any nightly looters or robberies. Additionally, while poor roofing might fail to give people shade or shelter, this tree will not. The trees can serve as a gathering place where everyone can safely enjoy themselves in a positive, outdoor environment and focus on a common goal.
